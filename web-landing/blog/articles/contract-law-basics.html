Understanding Contract Law: A Beginner's Guide

Everything you need to know about contracts in Nigeria - from formation to enforcement, written in plain English.

What is a Contract?

A contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties. When properly formed, a contract creates rights and obligations that the law will enforce. Simply put: if you make a valid contract and break it, the other party can take you to court.

Contracts are fundamental to modern life - you enter into them every day, often without realizing it:

  • Buying goods at a market or store
  • Hiring a taxi or ride-sharing service
  • Renting an apartment
  • Accepting a job offer
  • Taking out a loan
  • Subscribing to services (Netflix, internet, etc.)

Elements of a Valid Contract

Not every agreement is a legally enforceable contract. For a contract to be valid under Nigerian law, it must have these essential elements:

1. Offer

One party must make a clear, definite proposal to another. For example: "I will sell you my laptop for ‚Ç¶150,000."

Key point: The offer must be specific and communicated to the other party.

2. Acceptance

The other party must accept the offer exactly as made, without modifications. "Yes, I'll buy your laptop for ‚Ç¶150,000."

Key point: Changing the terms creates a counter-offer, not acceptance.

3. Consideration

Each party must give something of value. This is usually money, but can be goods, services, or a promise to do (or not do) something.

Key point: "I promise to give you ‚Ç¶1 million" is NOT a contract unless you get something in return.

4. Intention to Create Legal Relations

The parties must intend for the agreement to be legally binding. Business agreements are presumed to have this intent; family/social agreements generally don't.

Example: Agreeing to meet a friend for lunch is NOT a contract. Agreeing to supply goods to a business IS a contract.

5. Capacity

Both parties must have the legal ability to enter into a contract. This means:

  • Being of sound mind
  • Not being a minor (under 18 in Nigeria, with some exceptions)
  • Not being under undue influence or duress

6. Legality

The contract's purpose must be legal. You cannot enforce a contract to do something illegal.

Example: A contract to traffic drugs or evade taxes is void and unenforceable.

Types of Contracts

By Form

  • Written Contracts: Documented in writing and signed by parties
  • Oral Contracts: Verbal agreements (valid but harder to prove)
  • Implied Contracts: Created by conduct, not words (e.g., when you board a bus, you impliedly agree to pay the fare)

By Nature

  • Bilateral Contracts: Both parties exchange promises (most common)
  • Unilateral Contracts: One party makes a promise in exchange for an act (e.g., reward offers)

Common Business Contracts in Nigeria

  • Employment contracts
  • Tenancy agreements
  • Sales and purchase agreements
  • Service contracts
  • Partnership agreements
  • Loan agreements
  • Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs)

Does a Contract Have to Be in Writing?

Generally, no - oral contracts are valid in Nigeria. However, certain contracts MUST be in writing by law:

  • Contracts involving land (sale, lease for more than 3 years)
  • Contracts that cannot be performed within one year
  • Contracts of guarantee
  • Bills of exchange and promissory notes

Breach of Contract

A breach occurs when one party fails to fulfill their contractual obligations without lawful excuse. Breaches can be:

Types of Breach

  • Material Breach: Serious failure that defeats the contract's purpose (e.g., seller delivers completely different goods)
  • Minor Breach: Partial or insignificant failure (e.g., delivery is 2 days late when time wasn't essential)
  • Anticipatory Breach: One party declares in advance they won't perform (e.g., "I'm not going to pay you")

Remedies for Breach

If someone breaches a contract with you, Nigerian law provides several remedies:

1. Damages (Money)

The most common remedy. The court orders the breaching party to pay money to compensate for losses caused by the breach.

Example: You paid ‚Ç¶500,000 for goods that were never delivered. The seller must refund ‚Ç¶500,000 plus any additional losses you suffered.

2. Specific Performance

The court orders the breaching party to actually perform what they promised.

When used: Typically for unique items (land, rare goods) where money alone won't suffice.

3. Injunction

A court order preventing someone from doing something they promised not to do.

Example: Enforcing a non-compete clause preventing an ex-employee from working for a competitor.

4. Rescission

The contract is cancelled and parties are returned to their pre-contract positions.

When used: Often when the contract was formed through fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake.

Common Contract Mistakes to Avoid

The Rule: You're bound by what you sign, even if you didn't read it.

Solution: ALWAYS read every document before signing. If you don't understand it, get help.

‚ùå Relying on Verbal Promises

The Problem: Hard to prove, easy to deny.

Solution: Get it in writing. Even a simple email confirmation works.

‚ùå Vague or Ambiguous Terms

The Problem: "I'll pay you soon" or "deliver the goods quickly" - what does this mean exactly?

Solution: Be specific about amounts, dates, quantities, quality standards, etc.

‚ùå No Exit Clause

The Problem: Trapped in a contract with no way out.

Solution: Include clear termination clauses specifying how and when either party can end the contract.

‚ùå Ignoring Small Print

The Problem: Important terms are often in fine print.

Solution: Pay special attention to sections on liability, termination, and dispute resolution.

Essential Contract Clauses

Well-drafted contracts should include these key clauses:

  • Parties: Full names and addresses of all parties
  • Date and Duration: When the contract starts and ends
  • Obligations: What each party must do (be specific!)
  • Payment Terms: How much, when, and how payment is made
  • Warranties: Promises about quality, ownership, etc.
  • Limitation of Liability: Caps on damages for certain breaches
  • Confidentiality: Protection of sensitive information
  • Termination: How the contract can be ended
  • Dispute Resolution: How conflicts will be resolved (court, arbitration, mediation)
  • Governing Law: Which jurisdiction's laws apply
  • Entire Agreement: States that the written document contains the whole agreement

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I back out of a contract I just signed?

Generally, no - a contract is binding once formed. However, exceptions exist for certain consumer contracts (cooling-off periods), contracts formed under duress or fraud, or if there's a specific termination clause.

Is a WhatsApp or email agreement legally binding?

Yes! If all elements of a valid contract are present (offer, acceptance, consideration, etc.), electronic communications can form binding contracts. Keep records of all such exchanges.

What if the other party isn't performing but hasn't technically breached yet?

If you have reasonable grounds to believe they won't perform, you may be able to take action for "anticipatory breach." Consult a lawyer immediately.

Do I need a lawyer to draft every contract?

For simple, low-value transactions, templates may suffice. However, for significant business deals, employment agreements, or complex arrangements, professional legal advice is worth the investment.

Can I sue if someone breaks an oral contract?

Yes, oral contracts are enforceable. However, you'll need evidence (witnesses, text messages, emails, payment records) to prove the agreement existed and its terms.
