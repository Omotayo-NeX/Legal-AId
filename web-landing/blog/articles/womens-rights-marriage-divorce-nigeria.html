Women's Rights in Marriage and Divorce: A Complete Guide for Nigerian Women

Know your legal rights in marriage, understand divorce procedures, property division, child custody, and financial protections.

Legal Framework: The Matrimonial Causes Act

In Nigeria, marriage and divorce are primarily governed by the Matrimonial Causes Act (for statutory marriages) and various customary/Islamic laws (for customary and Islamic marriages). This guide focuses on statutory marriages but also addresses key issues affecting all Nigerian women.

üéØ Know This

Both husband and wife have the legal right to seek divorce in Nigeria if the marriage has broken down irretrievably. Gender does not determine who can file.

Grounds for Divorce in Nigeria

Under Section 15 of the Matrimonial Causes Act, there is only ONE ground for divorce: the marriage has broken down irretrievably.

Evidence of Irretrievable Breakdown (You must prove ONE of these):

  • Adultery ‚Äì Your spouse committed adultery and you find it intolerable to live with them
  • Unreasonable Behavior ‚Äì Your spouse behaves in a way that you cannot reasonably be expected to live with them
  • Desertion ‚Äì Your spouse deserted you for at least one continuous year
  • Separation (2 years) ‚Äì You've lived apart for 2+ years and both parties consent to divorce
  • Separation (3 years) ‚Äì You've lived apart for 3+ years (no consent needed)
  • Failure to Comply ‚Äì Your spouse has failed to comply with a decree of restitution of conjugal rights

Property Rights: The Hard Truth

One of the biggest challenges Nigerian women face in divorce is property division. The current legal reality is disappointing:

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Reality

Nigerian courts generally do not order equal redistribution of property at divorce. Property typically goes to whoever has legal title (name on the documents).

Section 72 of the Matrimonial Causes Act

While Section 72 grants courts power to make "just and equitable" property settlement orders, in practice:

  • Courts rarely use this power to redistribute property
  • Women must prove substantial financial contribution to property acquisition
  • Non-financial contributions (homemaking, child-rearing) are often undervalued
  • Property registered solely in the husband's name usually stays with him

Protecting Yourself:

Before and During Marriage:

  • Joint Ownership: Ensure property is registered in both names
  • Document Contributions: Keep records of financial contributions to property, businesses, and household
  • Prenuptial Agreements: Consider prenuptial agreements (increasingly recognized by Nigerian courts)
  • Maintain Financial Independence: Keep your own bank accounts and income sources
  • Business Partnerships: If contributing to spouse's business, formalize your role legally

Child Custody and Welfare

The paramount consideration in custody matters is the welfare and best interest of the child ‚Äì not the rights of parents.

Custody Types:

Type Description
Sole Custody One parent has physical custody and makes all decisions
Joint Custody Both parents share decision-making responsibilities
Visitation Rights Non-custodial parent has scheduled time with child

Factors Courts Consider:

  • Age of the child (young children often stay with mother)
  • Child's preference (if old enough to express a reasoned view)
  • Each parent's ability to provide for the child
  • Existing relationship between child and each parent
  • Stability of each parent's home environment
  • History of abuse or neglect

Financial Support: Maintenance and Alimony

Child Maintenance

Both parents have a legal duty to financially support their children, regardless of custody arrangements.

  • Courts can order the non-custodial parent to pay child support
  • Amount depends on the paying parent's income and child's needs
  • Covers food, clothing, education, healthcare, and shelter
  • Continues until child turns 18 (or 21 if in full-time education)

Spousal Maintenance (Alimony)

Nigerian courts can order spousal maintenance, but it's not automatic. Factors include:

  • Financial resources and needs of each spouse
  • Standard of living during the marriage
  • Age and health of both parties
  • Duration of the marriage
  • Contributions to the marriage (financial and non-financial)
  • Earning capacity and employment prospects

Divorce Procedure: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Filing the Petition

File a divorce petition at the High Court stating grounds for divorce.

Step 2: Serving Your Spouse

The court serves the petition on your spouse, who has time to respond.

Step 3: Decree Nisi

If grounds are proven and no defense succeeds, the court grants a conditional divorce order (Decree Nisi).

Step 4: Decree Absolute (Final)

After 3 months, you can apply for Decree Absolute, which finalizes the divorce.

Timeline:

Uncontested divorce: 6-12 months
Contested divorce: 1-3+ years

Special Protections for Women

Domestic Violence

Under the VAPP Act and state domestic violence laws:

  • You can obtain a protection order against an abusive spouse
  • Police must respond to domestic violence reports
  • Shelters and support services are available
  • Domestic violence is grounds for divorce

Economic Abuse

Denying you access to family finances, preventing you from working, or controlling all money can constitute economic abuse and unreasonable behavior.

Common Questions

Can I get a divorce if my husband refuses?

Yes. If you can prove the marriage has broken down irretrievably (through one of the grounds listed), you can obtain a divorce even without your husband's consent.

Will I get half of everything in divorce?

Unfortunately, not automatically. Nigerian courts don't typically order 50/50 division. You'll need strong evidence of contributions to claim property.

Can I take my children out of Nigeria during divorce?

Not without court permission or your spouse's written consent if custody is disputed. Doing so could constitute child abduction.

Do I need a lawyer to get divorced?

While not legally required, having a lawyer is highly recommended, especially for contested divorces or when property/custody is at issue.
