Tenant Rights and Rental Law in Nigeria: Complete Guide

Know your rights as a tenant - from tenancy agreements to eviction procedures, rent control, and landlord obligations.

Know Your Rights

Protecting Nigerian Tenants

Legal Framework

Rental law in Nigeria is governed by a combination of legislation including the Tenancy Law (varies by state), Recovery of Premises Act, and common law principles. Lagos and other states have their own specific tenancy laws.

Tenancy Agreements: What You Must Know

A tenancy agreement is a contract between landlord and tenant. While verbal agreements are valid, written agreements are strongly recommended.

Essential Terms in Your Agreement:

  • Rent amount - How much and when to pay
  • Tenancy duration - Fixed term or periodic
  • Security deposit - Amount and refund conditions
  • Permitted use - Residential or commercial
  • Maintenance responsibilities - Who fixes what
  • Notice periods - For termination or renewal

üí° Best Practice

Always read your tenancy agreement carefully before signing. Get a copy for your records. If anything is unclear, seek legal advice before committing.

Rent Payment and Increases

Advance Rent Payment

Nigerian landlords commonly demand rent in advance - sometimes for 1-2 years upfront. While legal, this practice creates significant financial burden on tenants.

In Lagos, the Tenancy Law 2011 limits advance rent to a maximum of 6 months for residential properties. Landlords cannot lawfully demand more than 6 months upfront.

Rent Increases

Landlords cannot arbitrarily increase rent during a fixed-term tenancy. Rent increases:

  • Must be agreed upon in the tenancy agreement
  • Can only occur at renewal (for fixed-term leases)
  • Must follow proper notice procedures
  • Should be reasonable and justified

Security Deposits

Security deposits (caution fees) are held to cover damages or unpaid rent.

Your Rights:

  • Deposit should be refunded in full if you leave the property in good condition
  • Landlord must provide evidence of damages to withhold deposit
  • Normal wear and tear is NOT grounds for withholding deposit
  • You're entitled to inspect the property together before moving out

Landlord Obligations

Your landlord has legal duties including:

Structural Maintenance

Landlords must maintain the building structure, roof, walls, foundations, and external areas.

Essential Services

Ensure water supply, electricity connections, and sanitation facilities are functional.

Quiet Enjoyment

Allow you to live peacefully without harassment or unannounced visits.

Safety

Ensure the property is safe and habitable - free from serious hazards.

Tenant Obligations

As a tenant, you must:

  • Pay rent on time as agreed
  • Keep the property clean and in good condition
  • Report damages and needed repairs promptly
  • Use the property properly according to the agreement
  • Not sublet without landlord's permission
  • Allow access for repairs (with reasonable notice)

Eviction Procedures: Know Your Protections

Landlords CANNOT forcibly evict you without following proper legal procedures. Self-help eviction (changing locks, removing belongings, cutting utilities) is illegal.

Lawful Eviction Process:

Step Requirement
1. Notice to Quit Written notice (period depends on tenancy type)
2. Demand for Possession 7 days after Notice to Quit expires
3. Court Proceedings File suit for possession in court
4. Court Order Obtain judgment for possession
5. Execution Court bailiff enforces eviction order

Notice Periods:

  • Monthly tenancy: 1 month notice
  • Quarterly tenancy: 3 months notice
  • Yearly tenancy: 6 months notice

Illegal Eviction: Your Rights

If your landlord attempts to evict you without following proper procedures, you can:

  • Report to the police - it's a criminal offense
  • Seek a court injunction to stop the eviction
  • Sue for damages for unlawful eviction
  • Re-enter the property if illegally locked out

What to Do If Things Go Wrong

Landlord Won't Make Repairs

1. Put your repair request in writing
2. Give reasonable time to comply
3. If urgent, make repairs and deduct from rent (document everything)
4. As last resort, seek court order for specific performance

Landlord Won't Refund Deposit

1. Send formal written demand
2. Provide evidence of property condition
3. File small claims action in court if necessary

Rent Dispute

1. Review your tenancy agreement
2. Attempt mediation or negotiation
3. Seek advice from Rent Tribunal (where available)
4. Consider legal action if unresolved

Common Questions

Can my landlord enter my apartment anytime?

No. You have a right to "quiet enjoyment." Landlords must give reasonable notice (typically 24 hours) except in emergencies.

What if my landlord increases rent mid-tenancy?

For fixed-term leases, rent cannot be increased until renewal unless the agreement specifically allows it. You can challenge unreasonable increases.

Can I withhold rent if landlord won't make repairs?

Risky strategy. Better to document the issue, make repairs yourself if urgent, and deduct the cost with receipts. Consult a lawyer first.

What's the difference between Notice to Quit and eviction?

Notice to Quit is just the first step. Eviction requires a court order. Landlords cannot forcibly remove you without court involvement.
