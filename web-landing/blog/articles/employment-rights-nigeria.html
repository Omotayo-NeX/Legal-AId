Employment Rights in Nigeria: Essential Guide for Workers

Know your rights as an employee in Nigeria - from hiring to termination, leave entitlements to workplace protections.

Worker Rights

Protecting Nigerian Employees

Legal Framework

Nigerian employment law is governed by the Labour Act (Cap L1 LFN 2004), various regulations, and common law principles. Understanding your rights helps protect you from exploitation and unfair treatment.

Employment Contracts

Every employment relationship should be governed by a contract - written or implied. Key elements:

  • Job title and description
  • Salary and benefits
  • Working hours and location
  • Leave entitlements
  • Termination procedures
  • Notice periods

üí° Best Practice

Always get your employment terms in writing. Even if your employer doesn't provide a formal contract, keep emails, offer letters, and any written communication about your job.

Leave Entitlements

Annual Leave

Under the Labour Act, employees are entitled to:

  • At least 12 working days of paid annual leave after 12 months of continuous service
  • Some employers offer more generous leave (15-30 days)
  • Leave must be taken - it cannot be forfeited
  • Employers must pay full salary during leave

Maternity Leave

Female employees are entitled to:

  • 12 weeks (3 months) of maternity leave
  • At least 6 weeks must be taken after childbirth
  • Full pay for at least 50% of the leave period
  • Job protection - you cannot be fired for taking maternity leave

Sick Leave

While not specifically mandated by the Labour Act, most employers provide sick leave. Check your employment contract or company policy.

Working Hours and Overtime

  • Maximum: 8 hours per day or 40 hours per week
  • Overtime: Work beyond normal hours should be compensated at higher rates
  • Rest periods: Employees must have at least one day off per week
  • Public holidays: 12 public holidays with full pay

Termination of Employment

Notice Requirements

Length of Service Minimum Notice
Less than 3 months 1 day
3 months to 2 years 1 week
2 to 5 years 2 weeks
5+ years 1 month

Wrongful Termination

You may have a claim if terminated:

  • Without proper notice or payment in lieu
  • For discriminatory reasons (gender, religion, ethnicity)
  • For exercising legal rights (e.g., taking maternity leave)
  • In breach of your employment contract

Severance Pay

Upon redundancy or termination without cause, you may be entitled to severance pay based on years of service and your employment contract.

Workplace Safety

Employers have a legal duty to provide:

  • Safe working environment
  • Proper equipment and training
  • First aid facilities
  • Protection from harassment and discrimination

What to Do If Your Rights Are Violated

Step 1: Document Everything

Keep records of contracts, emails, payslips, and incidents.

Step 2: Internal Resolution

Raise the issue with your HR department or supervisor formally in writing.

Step 3: Ministry of Labour

File a complaint with your state's Ministry of Labour for mediation.

Step 4: Industrial Arbitration Panel / National Industrial Court

If unresolved, take legal action through appropriate labor tribunals.

Common Questions

Can my employer fire me without notice?

Generally, no - unless it's for gross misconduct (theft, fraud, violence). For ordinary termination, proper notice or payment in lieu is required.

Am I entitled to a written contract?

While best practice, the law doesn't strictly require a written contract. However, the terms of employment must be communicated to you clearly.

Can my employer reduce my salary?

Not unilaterally. Salary reductions require your consent or must be part of a proper redundancy/restructuring process with notice.
