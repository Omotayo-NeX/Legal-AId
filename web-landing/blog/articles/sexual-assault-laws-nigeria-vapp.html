Understanding Sexual Assault Laws in Nigeria: The VAPP Act Explained

Know your rights and legal protections under Nigeria's Violence Against Persons (Prohibition) Act 2015.

Legal Protection Against Violence

What is the VAPP Act?

The Violence Against Persons (Prohibition) Act 2015 (VAPP Act) is Nigeria's most comprehensive legislation addressing sexual violence and assault. Signed into law by President Goodluck Jonathan in May 2015, it provides crucial legal protections and criminalizes various forms of violence.

What Constitutes Sexual Assault Under the VAPP Act?

The Act defines rape as intentional penetration of the vagina, anus, or mouth of another person with any part of the body or anything else, without consent.

Forms of Sexual Violence Covered:

  • Rape (including marital rape)
  • Sexual assault and harassment
  • Indecent exposure
  • Forced sexual initiation
  • Incest
  • Spousal battery

Penalties for Sexual Assault

Offense Penalty
Rape (Adult Victim) Life imprisonment
Rape (Child Under 14) Minimum 12 years imprisonment
Gang Rape Minimum 20 years (each offender)
Sexual Harassment Up to 3 years or fine or both

Sex Offenders Register

One of the most significant provisions of the VAPP Act is the establishment of a National Sex Offenders Register. When convicted, offenders' details are entered into this register, which:

  • Helps prevent repeat offenses
  • Assists law enforcement in tracking offenders
  • Provides public protection information
  • Is maintained by NAPTIP (National Agency for the Prohibition of Trafficking in Persons)

Victim Rights and Protections

If You Are a Victim:

  • Free Medical Treatment: Victims are entitled to free medical treatment
  • Privacy Protection: Your identity is protected during legal proceedings
  • Compensation: Courts can order compensation for victims
  • Protection Orders: You can obtain court orders for protection
  • No Reconciliation Requirement: Cases cannot be settled or withdrawn due to reconciliation

How to Report Sexual Assault

Step 1: Ensure Your Safety

Move to a safe location away from the perpetrator immediately.

Step 2: Seek Medical Care

Visit a hospital for:

  • Medical treatment for injuries
  • Evidence collection (within 72 hours is crucial)
  • Prevention of STIs and pregnancy
  • Documentation for legal proceedings

Step 3: Report to Authorities

You can report to:

  • Police: Any police station can take your report
  • NAPTIP: National Agency for the Prohibition of Trafficking in Persons
  • Gender-Based Violence Response Units: Available in major cities

State Adoption of VAPP Act

As of 2025, 29 out of 36 states have adopted the VAPP Act. The Act automatically applies in the Federal Capital Territory (Abuja). Check with Legal AI.d or local authorities to confirm if your state has adopted the Act.

States with VAPP Adoption Include:

Lagos, Ogun, Ekiti, Anambra, Ebonyi, Osun, Ondo, Rivers, and others. Lagos and Ekiti have their own comprehensive legislation aligned with VAPP principles.

Common Myths Debunked

Myth: "Husbands cannot rape their wives"

FALSE. The VAPP Act criminalizes marital rape. Marriage does NOT grant automatic consent for sexual activity.

Myth: "Only women can be raped"

FALSE. The VAPP Act recognizes that anyone, regardless of gender, can be a victim of rape or sexual assault.

Myth: "Victims must fight back to prove assault"

FALSE. Lack of physical resistance does NOT mean consent. Fear, shock, and survival instincts often prevent fighting back.

Myth: "Cases can be settled out of court"

FALSE. Sexual violence is a criminal offense against the state. Cases cannot be withdrawn due to reconciliation or settlement.
