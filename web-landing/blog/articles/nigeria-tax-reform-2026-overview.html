Nigeria Tax Reform Acts 2026: What You Need to Know Before January

President Tinubu signed the most comprehensive tax reform legislation since 1999. Here's everything you need to know about the changes coming January 1, 2026.

Overview of the Tax Reform

On June 26, 2025, President Bola Ahmed Tinubu signed into law the Nigeria Tax Act, 2025 (NTA), alongside three complementary bills that will fundamentally reshape Nigeria's tax landscape. This reform represents the most significant overhaul of the country's tax structure in over two decades and will take effect on January 1, 2026.

The four key pieces of legislation include:

  • Nigeria Tax Act, 2025
  • Nigeria Revenue Service (Establishment) Bill
  • Nigeria Tax Administration Bill
  • Joint Revenue Board (Establishment) Bill

The reform aims to simplify Nigeria's tax system, provide relief to low-income earners, increase revenue collection efficiency, and align Nigeria with global tax standards. Most changes become effective January 1, 2026.

Major Changes to Personal Income Tax

One of the most significant impacts of the tax reform will be felt in personal income taxation. The new law introduces a progressive tax structure designed to provide substantial relief to low and middle-income earners while ensuring higher-income individuals contribute proportionally more.

Tax Exemption for Minimum Wage Earners

A landmark provision of the reform is the full tax exemption for minimum wage earners with an annual income of ‚Ç¶800,000 or less. This means:

  • Approximately 90% of workers in the private sector will be exempted from paying income tax
  • Low-income earners will have more disposable income
  • Compliance burden on small employers will be reduced

Progressive Tax Bands

For those earning above ‚Ç¶800,000 annually, a new progressive tax structure will apply, with rates increasing based on income levels. This ensures that higher earners contribute more while protecting the purchasing power of average Nigerians.

Corporate Tax Transformations

Businesses should prepare for significant changes in corporate taxation:

Reduced Corporate Tax Rates

  • 2025 Year of Assessment: 27.5% for all companies (except small companies)
  • From 2026 onwards: 25% corporate tax rate

New Definition of "Small Company"

The tax reform redefines what qualifies as a small company:

  • Gross turnover of ‚Ç¶50,000,000 or less per annum
  • Total fixed assets not exceeding ‚Ç¶250,000,000
  • Important: Professional services firms do NOT qualify regardless of revenue

Small companies enjoy preferential tax treatment, making this classification crucial for business planning.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT) Overhaul

The tax reform brings dramatic changes to capital gains taxation:

Tripled CGT Rate for Companies

The CGT rate for companies has been revised from 10% to 30% - a 200% increase. This significant jump requires careful tax planning for businesses contemplating asset sales or restructuring.

Expanded CGT Base

CGT now applies to previously untaxed assets, including:

  • Cryptocurrencies and digital assets
  • Patents and intellectual property
  • Foreign exchange gains
  • Rights and debts
  • Incorporeal assets

Value Added Tax (VAT) Updates

While the VAT rate remains at 7.5%, the administration and application of VAT has been modernized:

Input VAT Claims Expanded

Nigeria now adopts globally recognized VAT principles that allow businesses to claim input VAT on:

  • All purchases (not just goods)
  • Services
  • Fixed assets

This change improves cash flow for VAT-registered businesses and eliminates the tax cascade effect that previously inflated costs.

Institutional Reforms: From FIRS to NRS

The Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS) is being transformed into the Nigeria Revenue Service (NRS) with enhanced capabilities:

  • Greater autonomy: More independence in operations and decision-making
  • Digital-first approach: Modern, technology-driven tax administration
  • Expanded mandate: Collection of both tax and non-tax revenues
  • Improved efficiency: Streamlined processes and better taxpayer services

New Tax Incentive Framework

The tax reform replaces the Pioneer Status Incentive with the Economic Development Tax Incentive (EDTI), featuring:

  • Credit-based framework tied to actual investment size
  • Sector-priority alignment with national development goals
  • Stricter qualification criteria to prevent abuse
  • Performance-based benefits

Global Minimum Tax Implementation

Aligning with international standards, Nigeria will implement a minimum effective tax rate (ETR) of 15% for:

  • Large multinational groups
  • Companies with Nigerian operations meeting size thresholds

This ensures that large corporations pay a fair minimum tax rate, preventing aggressive tax planning that erodes the tax base.

What You Should Do Before January 2026

To prepare for the tax reform, individuals and businesses should take these steps:

For Individuals:

  1. Calculate your tax position: Determine if you fall within the ‚Ç¶800,000 exemption threshold
  2. Review your investment portfolio: Assess potential CGT implications on cryptocurrencies and other newly-taxable assets
  3. Update withholding tax information: Ensure your employer has correct tax details
  4. Plan year-end transactions: Consider timing of asset sales to optimize tax position

For Businesses:

  1. Conduct a tax health check: Review your company's classification (small company vs. regular company)
  2. Assess CGT exposure: Identify assets that may trigger the new 30% CGT rate
  3. Optimize VAT processes: Ensure systems can track and claim input VAT on services and fixed assets
  4. Review tax incentives: Determine if your business qualifies for the new EDTI framework
  5. Update accounting systems: Ensure software can handle new tax rates and categories
  6. Train finance teams: Educate staff on new compliance requirements

Potential Challenges and Considerations

While the tax reform brings many improvements, there are potential challenges to anticipate:

Implementation Timeline

With only weeks until the January 1, 2026 effective date, businesses may face tight timelines to update systems, train staff, and ensure compliance.

Transition Period Complexity

The shift from old to new tax regimes may create confusion, particularly for:

  • Transactions straddling year-end
  • Multi-year contracts
  • Existing tax disputes

Digital Infrastructure Requirements

The NRS's digital-first approach requires robust IT infrastructure. Businesses and individuals should ensure they have the necessary digital capabilities for e-filing and online compliance.

Frequently Asked Questions

When exactly do the tax reforms take effect?

The Nigeria Tax Act, 2025 and related bills take effect on January 1, 2026. Some specific provisions may have different effective dates - check with a tax professional for details on particular provisions.

Will I be taxed on my cryptocurrency holdings?

Cryptocurrencies are now subject to Capital Gains Tax when you sell or dispose of them. Simply holding cryptocurrency is not taxable, but any gains realized upon sale will be taxed at the applicable CGT rate (30% for companies).

If your annual income is ‚Ç¶800,000 or less, you are fully exempt from personal income tax under the new law. This applies from January 1, 2026.

Can I still claim input VAT on business purchases?

Yes! In fact, the new law expands your ability to claim input VAT. You can now claim VAT on services and fixed assets, not just goods - bringing Nigeria in line with global best practices.

Conclusion: A New Era of Taxation in Nigeria

The Nigeria Tax Reform Acts of 2025 represent a bold step toward modernizing Nigeria's tax system. While change always brings challenges, these reforms offer significant opportunities:

  • Reduced tax burden for low and middle-income earners
  • More competitive corporate tax rates
  • Simplified VAT compliance
  • Improved tax administration through digitalization
  • Alignment with global tax standards

Success in navigating these changes requires proactive preparation. Whether you're an individual taxpayer or a business owner, the time to act is now - before January 2026 arrives.
